Iowa City: Discerning Trends in Park Type Across Iowa City Park Districts

Over the past decade, Iowa City has invested heavily into its public park system. Through the use of documents such as the Iowa City Parks Master Plan, Natural Areas Inventory and Management Plan, and the Urban Forestry Management Plan, Parks and Rec staff have sought to create a diverse and equitable parks system. Inspiration for this web based geographic visualization originates with the “District Analysis” from the Parks Master Plan. The parks system is divided into six districts: North, Central, Southeast, South, West Central, and West. Each District contains 8-12 parks except for the West district with 1 park only.The district analysis in the Parks Master Plan portrays each district individually, but lacks a citywide district comparison with respect to the types of parks in each district.

The objective of this project is to compare the different districts as a whole with respect to acres of park type per 1000 residents; the map and parallel coordinate plot below give the user a multivariate tool to compare park districts. Parks offer an essential role in providing space for exercise, relaxation, play, and help define the character of a neighborhood. Iowa City has expressed commitment in increasing equity and enhancing the park system, and perhaps this project could act as an aid in doing so.

Park character is the primary attribute of a park, and 8 parks characters are defined by the City of Iowa City as such:

  • “Compete”: This park is set up to foster competitive sports, with multiple ballfields and perhaps a concession stand and bleachers
  • “Connect”: These parks support social connections through programming or facilities
  • “Go Wild”: Nature preservation is the focus of these parks, and natural areas with native plants and animals shape the human experience
  • “Learn”: Parks that promote outdoor learning spaces, science-based activities or integration of the arts fit in this class of parks
  • “Move”: This category includes trails and parks that are primarily meant to get people walking, jogging or hiking
  • “Play”: Typically residents will find manicured open space, a shelter, and some limited programmed recreation space (e.g., a basketball court or a ball field)
  • “Splash”: Water play is the dominant theme here—specifically, constructed water play (splash pads) rather than a creek or river
  • “Reflect”: These parks provide opportunities to pull away from the hustle and bustle and to de-stress

In addition to the park characters, we included total acres per 1000 residents and a simple socio-economic scale (1= low income and low education, 5= high income and high education). These two categories offer an analysis tool to discern whether park characters are typical of a given socio-economic rank.
